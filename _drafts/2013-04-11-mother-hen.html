Blue electric light curves into the sky from metallic, fishy skyscrapers. An ambient haze paints the west; what was red mist is now purple penumbra. Street lamps shed cones of white onto brick footpath, illuminating mosquitoes. The tiles are blue and lay in pairs, twisting and tessellating into diamonds that checker down the walkway. The wind is a whisper that flows out to sea... well, not really, it's carrying a child's scream as he descends full-tilt down a grassy knoll, gelatinous knees pumping furiously.
A hive of youngsters clamor about the jungle gym. They are giggling, and crying and sweating and yelling and eating dirt as a mother leaves with her boy. She clops along with a masculine swag, buxom hips jaunting above high-heeled sandals. Dirty blonde hair and a slavic-ridge to the nose. She slides two fallen bra-straps beneath her black leather vest as the boy is moaning, and intermittently pausing -- then sprinting to catch up with a wail. He has black skin and wiry spires of hair exploding from his little skull. A puffy "Cowboys" jacket ruffles with each stride as he runs with the haphazard knee-lift of the newly supra-ambulatory toddler: he can't help   goose-stepping.
The boy and the mother are followed by a meek woman of brown complexion and a soft chin, tucked firmly into her chest. Her face and body are covered in blue abaya. She pushes a gaudy plastic stroller shaped like a red race car. The wheels rattle like rolling maracas.
The boy runs up to his mother and grunts. Mother hen turns.

'do you understand me?'
The boy stares on, indignant and patient, arms outstretched. She crouches in a baseball catcher position and grabs his cheeks,
'I vil' not carry you!'
A moan. Bubbling dissent.
'No.'
More moans.
'No.'
The boy takes her arms and places them around him, enveloping himself and wriggling into her chest.
'No! Do you understand me! You stupid piece of sheet' I vil' not carry you!'
She shoves his little body away and whines. The nanny sits idly, tapping the stroller, cooing.
'No. If you keep on crying like that I vil' smack'joo' so hart' you will not be able to walk!'
The boy stares and stamps his feet.
She stands up and walks away, eliciting a scream and a sprint. He grabs hold of her billowing khaki's and pulls fiercely.
'Damnit no! You weigh fivteen' kilos! I hev' carried'joo so much, I have pain!'
He howls and tugs to no avail. The red race car rattles up beside and rocks back and forth, like an eager horse.
'No. Look. You hev' a nice, expensive car. Ride in the fucking car.'
The nanny beckons. She softly places her hand on the boy's hips; pulls him into the car. He screams and squirms out of the plastic throne, sprints again towards a receding mother.
'God damnit! You leetle' shit! Geet' in the car. I'm tired.'
Tears fall on bricks, sinking between cracks. His arms tremble as he pulls on the fluffy, iridescent jacket in anguish. A final reaching upward of the hands, fingertips outstretched, eyes tightly closed; he convokes his mother's affection: she clops away with a toss of the mane. He lets loose a middle-eastern lament.
'No.'
And so the cortege continues. The howling boy, sprinting, tugging, pulling, watching. The nanny crooning and tapping the car. The frowsy leather-vested mother leading the way, haughty and horrible, cigarette between lips.
