I've got a cramp in my neck that's reminiscent of Halo 2 headshots, sniper rifles, screaming roommates, a beer stained futon, and a broken XBox360; oh, and peanut-butter-dipped Oreos. Can't forget those peanut-butter-dipped Oreos, which were consumed religiously at 2:00am…

Such were the sensory details of my freshman year -- of college[1]-- but this cramp wasn't caused by video games and the incredible stress of looking for Rocket Launcher Ammunition while some douchebag is hunting you down in the ‘flying-fag-ship’, the Banshee; no, it was caused by 7 hours of coding. And by coding, I mean relentless error messages.

I had a roommate named Matt Hale, who was an absolute stud in Halo – and in life apparently, he 'grew up' to be the captain of the Water Polo team and graduated an engineer, although we had very much fallen out of touch by then – he could kill across the map with a no-scope, or hit you with a sticky plasma grenade by sheer telekinesis. He exerted his will upon that Xbox360 – I am convinced of it. He encouraged me when I was dying with great frequency, holding back our four-person-team during capture the flag matches. I ran around those digital maps of gravity lifts and teleports and alien weaponry like a headless chicken, throwing myself onto the nearest murderer, useful only to up the kill count for the other side. This failure to my teammates caused me great distress.  Matt Hale laughed and told me to chill out.

Despite incessant practicing late into the night, often at the expense of Physics problem sets and papers, I could not attain the desired skill and fluidity with those XboX360 controllers. Those damned grey joysticks and their peremptory requirement of dexterous – and calloused – thumbs.  I was a lost cause and despite all my stress and anxiety, the many deep cramps I created in my right shoulder before track practice – thereby crippling myself for workouts and runs, garnering the pathos and commentary of teammates who thought I was under some sort of mortal emotional strain outside of class[2]– I wasn’t able improve.

And so it goes with coding. I am not a good programmer: I am just stubborn; and, emotionally invested. I was asked to the play the coding game for hours upon hours upon hours[3]when I departed on a Senor thesis project.  C Makefilesand headerfiles and source files and binary executables and library archives and bash scripts and all sorts of horrible rigmarole that encompassed my Senior year.  What a turn around: same cramp, more productive.

Beginning in September, I applied all the force of my mind to the keyboard in the basement of Peyton hall, trying to figure out banal function declarations in python and converting astrophysical coordinates to Cartesian. I scoured SDSSdocumentation at a frenetic pace, retaining absolutely no information and probably a net negative inference of understanding. All I did was pull my bangs further and further out from my forehead. With each passing hour in that awful basement, before I had figured things out, I was very much like the poor blue-helmeted major-sergeant who was murdered rapidly before he could even get his paws on a battle rifle. I was digitally cavorting around and crying about it like a child.

But things got better with time. Over the course of my freshman year, I began to relax – about Halo. I began to not care as much, and played with students of a lower caliber. One game I got 28 headshotsin a row[4]. I noticed that with less stress, and more calm intuition, things begin to work out for you. It's all a question of not getting too worked up about it. I began to feel a sense of efficacy with those whirling joysticks – the right of rifle, left of waist – pointing my battle rifle across maps, effusing enfilades, and inspiring moans of frustration from the futon-seatmate to my right (or left, the point is they were the enemy). And so by the end of the year I was a decent Halo player.

If only I could do the same with coding. 

There are times when it is a good idea to code: when you're feeling particularly fastidious, persnickety, or annoyingly logical. But that's not all the time for me. I tend to think in ephemeral abstraction, fleeting sentiment, and rapidly flowing illusions of barely manifest inklings… I'm a bit of a dreamer. I have great difficulty using words to represent my mind – the situation gets worse with static private functions of instances of objects[5]

And so it still goes with coding, in my world. I continue to play until I am over-saturated, until my retinas are practically singed off and I can no longer form a sentence because the heap-stack calls and pointers-to-functions and IOExceptions and luminescent green gobbledygook have radiated so fiercely on my computer screen as to coagulate my Broca’s area  into an insentient slime.[6]But anyway, it's clear I haven't learned my lesson. I'm sitting here, after eating a wonderful meal of sushi and mango maki, still uncomfortable, not able to relax and read a book or go see friends because that damn error message is still haunting me, and the computer that is designed to solve it is sitting a half mile away… The temptation is great to go out of my door, right now, and pick up that damn keyboard and get rid of all those index-array-out-of-bounds messages: but I'm trying to exercise some willpower and maturity right now – I’m gonna’ stay off the juice. [7]

Perhaps in the morning I'll be more relaxed, and like Halo, things will come easier with calm intuition rather then extreme strain and focus. It's amazing how deep thought turns into a very physical process. When you push your physical bounds in any regime, intra or extra-cranial, you've got to be in the zone. Ugh. That was cute phrase to end on. Not.
