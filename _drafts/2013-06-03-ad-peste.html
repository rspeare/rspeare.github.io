A stodgy haze hangs over the city. It is encased in a soporific, stagnant mist: a bell jar. Schedules there are tentative, even ephemeral. There are no set dates. Diurnal ambient smog smudges itself between skyscrapers and occludes any attempted vision above street level. Unlike other cities, there is no visual contemplation of the sky between steel fingers; its colors and gradients and textures are too coarse to be beautiful – as if they were drawn on blue canvas by a child's crayon. By night, electric blue and green halos pierce through penumbra, luring Bedouins to empty streets.
By day, pedestrians of varying sizes, shapes, smells and color ambulate beneath a moist sun. Indian men in checkered oxford shirts with draped sweat stains between their shoulders exude strong body door. Kandora-clad men float like wraiths and effervesce perfumes and piquant artificial zests that sting nasal hair roots. Immaculately trimmed beards line the faces of young Arab men, never without Aviator sunglasses. Women often carry two cell phones: one for private calls, one for the husband. Expats and imported middle class white workers walk the city in wanderlust dream, sweating through business jackets or click stepping in Khaki shorts and Sperry's. Filipinos, dressed in company wear, walk the street in throngs and couples and as single men and women, dominating the office with their numbers and offering an eager congeniality to any edifice they occupy. Young boys, dressed in 80's hip wear and cropped retro haircuts, cut across 4 lane highways, asking 50mph-translating taxis – with their palms – to  stop for their presumptuous crossing. Middle aged men high step along the sidewalks, exercising in casual clothes and doing push ups on Park benches. Skyscrapers tower out of the tiled cement, reaching into that unclear bog in the sky that sticks sand and muck to glass. At certain street corners, a damp hot wind drifts inland from the marina, and heartens pedestrians with nature’s movement. Because of so many large buildings, winnowing air from the sea is nearly impossible. The people stew in the shadowy leeway of their Air-conditioned luxury.
Families with sticky plastic backpacks and shopping bags carry their children across vast crosswalks, following the fluorescent instructions of one green speckled man. He is the life, the way, the guide to safety in the presence of rocketing cars and the psychotic melting pot that is the transit system of that City. Bentley's, Lamberginis, and suped BMW sports cars launch from stoplights beside run-down Hondas, mini vans, Mitsubishi pick up trucks and gigantic public transit buses. The sports sedans are occupied by rich expat business men or powerful young Arabs. The buses are packed chock full of Indian and Bengladeshi and Filipino workers; but they are a comfy comparison to the white blocky prisons that carry jumpsuit-clad construction workers between labor camps.  The run-down Hondas: Shop owners and hard-working family men – or, reckless youth – trying to make it in a city of so much speed and sumptuousness. The Taxis, driven by heavy foots to meet the daily quota of transportation fare.
Dr. R knew nothing of this world, and yet had come for the work; for the money. Medical bills were coming in for his daughter and it had been time to make a change. His wife and child were back in Allentown Pennsylvania, carrying on the daily logistic ritual, the extremely punctual procedure that was the upbringing of a teenage girl. Soccer Practice and Jazz Band, Cantata and Clarinet lessons, SAT tutoring and football games and travel leagues. Barbara and he had risen each morning at 6 o' clock like machinery in order to pound out Clara's daily schedule. There was no stop to the dedication and fierce love that they put into their daughter's growth and expansion, until one day, she had begun to feel weak. What started as a head cold became severe weakness; a trip to the doctors revealed two tumors lodged deep within her 16 year old lungs. The prognosis wasn't damning, but it was costly. Dropping out of school the next quarter, chemo, radiation – a horrifying rigmarole to save Clara.
The city had treated him well thus far. The job at the clinic was mundane and horribly inefficient, but his salary was generous and his room cleanly spacious. The soulless nature of his furnishings didn't bother him after a time; he began to accustom to the sterile off white walls and the dull geometric patterns of hotel apartment art. He spoke with his daughter after work, wishing her a good day at school, and spoke with Barbara every morning, at 5am, exchanging stories of their days – and days to be – attempting to stay in phase across an ocean. Clara's health had improved over the course of the fall. It was now December and her final exams were approaching. Principle Beth had accepted an appeal to take a lightened course load, and Clara was enjoying her preparation for one French exam and one Biology project. She was investigating the Bubonic Plague and the old Bacterial scourges of the past. A bit of hypochondriac her whole life, it was ironic the Clara had fallen ill to a disease that held no preferences between clean or unclean, was only a product of stochastic aberrance of genetic material over time – cancer had no cleanliness, it was a consequence of chaos, poorly re-written code, true human error.
R flipped on the TV and scrolled through Arabic news channels. Flash after flash of deliberately gesticulating Kandora-ed men, pantomiming the bucktoothed arbiters of CNN mass hysteria and MSNBC sentimentality abroad. He stopped upon an Arabic subtitled exhibition of Rocky IV and leaned back into his squirming, whining, black leather couch. The spacious but stiff cushions recalcitrantly enveloped him, and as R. lazily extended to his left hand to pick up a glass of Whiskey, he knocked over a container of Yoplait yogurt. 'The maid will clean it up tomorrow', he thought as he took a slug of amber liquid. 'God, that's an awful thought. What is this place doing to me?'. He got up off the couch, which screamed in delight as its stiff folds un-creased, and went to the kitchen for a paper towel. Unrolling a fresh sheet he picked up the Yoplait cup and slopped off the remains of one vanilla parfait from the cold white tiled floor. R was a bit of health nut. He had continued to follow the regiment of his college days, which stipulated a light dinner before bed and a large breakfast. In this city, it was easy to come upon extravagant pancakes, 50 dirham for three, if you were willing to take a cab and shell out substantial time, committing to a gustatory-centric start to your day. R was not much of cook. But thank god that didn't matter because groceries in the city were strange and un appealing to him. He just bought yogurt and oatmeal and loaves of bread and cheese, contenting himself to pagan snacks at home.
Barbara loved to cook and treated R through the years with an increasing stomach-solicitousness that had given him a tidy paunch, just above the belt line. Chocolate chip pancakes and eggs Florentine, R dined like a king because Barbara loved him in real ways; she was always willing to feed his early appetite – as long as he made the bed and took the responsibility of rousing Clara to shower. R loved those mornings at home. In his younger days he jogged a 3 mile loop around the block – at the initial beckoning of Barbara, who recognized no athletic talent whatsoever in her husband early on – a twisting maze of hills and sidewalks that had kept him in vigorous health for a number of years; but with the oncoming of Clara's sickness, he had grown lax, and the bouncing jauntiness of his instep had disappeared beneath the weight of concern and responsibility.. Bags now hung beneath his eyes as he watched Apollo Ono hit the Mat – downed by Drago the destroyer forever. He used the gym intermittently, and had taken to drinking two or three whiskey a night to replace a howling loneliness he had yet to recognize.
At home, Clara was picking up on her father's enervation; but could not tell Mom. She was the only one who saw him slur to the computer screen, ever so slightly, or laugh in a maniacal way at a not-so-funny detail of her week. By the time he woke up and talked to Mom, he must look a lot better.  Maybe a bit tired. He always looks so tired. She was unsure what to tell Mom, because they had both said so sternly that Dad’s move was absolutely necessary. But Mom didn’t see him after work. Worried and guilty and feeling horrible for letting a disease creep up inside her, Clara cried silently into the pillow every morning at 5:55am, just before her Father would come in to sit on her bed and rub her back softly
– Hey little one. Time for your day. Up and adam now sweetheart.
Her mom thought the stains on the pillow were drool or snot, apparently. She never said a thing. Clara could always clear her eyes and smile sufficiently by 8am, her new wake up time that was allowed by taking mid-morning and afternoon classes for Chemo. Her mother would step into the room, crack the door and say,
– Time get up Clara!
She would switch on the lights or walk over the window and open the shades, her practicality garish next to her father’s gentler routine. She missed him terribly and knew that he was coming to a bad end. The bed behind him on the computer screen always look ruffled and unmade, not a book or a painting or a chair in the place save his couch – which he had shown her multiple times – and empty glasses lining the bed stand. She hoped they were filled with water at night, for sleeping in that hot climate.
What Clara didn't realize was that those glasses were for water, and R. had been waking up each night in hot sweats, but it was because his air conditioning was sadistically erratic. There was no medium between the frigid 19C of “AC ON” and the concomitant 27C of “AC OFF”. The heat from his window infected his living space with rapacious delight. The entropy outside his little apartment was enormous; it insidiously roiled the gas particles and his dreaming thoughts into a turbid tormenting mess, given a temporary absence of AC.
Drago was now in the gym training, next to Montage images of Rocky lifting rocks and friends and cars and mountains. Well, he wasn't lifting the mountains, just running up them; In boots. R loved these split shot 80's euphoric statements of the cold war athleticism, the height of America's pride and eminence as the US 'won over' the waning Soviet power. These days victory was less clear. The recession had cleared out much of his bank account, accumulated over 20 years of practicing medicine in Pennsylvania. 2007-2008 alone cleared out 35% of his retirement funds. It felt like starting over. Clara was an athlete like her mother, and could have been up for a State School scholarship; but with the cancer, things were now doubtful. It was her Junior year and colleges were recruiting hard. R. doubted he could make a pity case to Penn State or Ohio or Georgetown, the coaches who had initially contacted Clara one year ago.
It was awful to see the slow diminution of Clara's body. Beneath the chemo, her once hard constitution was fading into a pallid soft complexion; making her initially beautiful and like a doll, but now haggard and seemingly senescent. He had a middle-aged woman for a daughter. Clara, with her thin hair and ghostly color, looked 40 rather than 16 sometimes when she sat in her room. The stuffed animals on her checkered bedspread, beneath billboards of the Shins and the Rolling Stones looked horribly out of place in his Computer screen caricature.
Barbara was still beautiful, and holding onto life with her extreme resilience, the rigorous energy that had so attracted R to her in college. A stud on the track team, Barbara amazed R. with the wanton aggression and grimace she displayed on the Mondo tarmac. He had seen Barbara crash and kick through hurdles, knocking down every single barrier, and still win the race beside more graceful competitors. It was like she wanted to knock down those hurdles. It wasn’t enough to win; she had to demolish both scene and participants. The maniacal rage, the blonde amazon he saw manifest on the track, struck fear and erotic curiosity in R. the first time he saw her. What would she be like in bed, this ferocious woman?
Clara had the same rage at times, but could only sustain it for brief moments. After a foul in the box during sophomore year playoffs – a kick the stomach, which took her out for a few minutes on the sideline – she returned to the field with a chilling silence that gave R goosebumps. Barbara had already proclaimed 'Get that bitch.', a little too loudly for R's comfort in the stands. (They were sitting on the away team's side, or the home's, he didn't know, it was an unclear travel game). After a turnover, Clara sprinted down the right field and caught the ball from a through pass. Cutting towards center and confronted with her perpetrator, she chose to feint and dive towards the goal line instead of passing, throwing her shoulder into the opponent made Barbara cry, 'OOOOooooOOhhh yeah baby!'. She bumped and twisted her way towards the corner post of the goal – giving her old defender a bloody nose in the process – and chipped it into the net with an indignant toe-ball, successful in her nemesis.
 And then she played demurely the rest of the game, embarrassed of the moral transgression.
'Did I do the right thing daddy? Hitting that girl? She was a bitch, but I didn't know my throwing my shoulder like that would hit her in the face.'
'it was fine sweetheart. She had already knocked your guts out. I was proud of you to take it to her.'
'GIVE IT TO ME DOOKIE!!!' Barbara leered. 'That was amazing Clara, don't apologize for using force where force is needed. That girl deserved it, and you showed some real power there! I bet coach Callahan was super impressed. I saw him look up at me and give me the thumbs up in the stands.'
'Is that true Daddy?'
'Yes, honey.'
R. Didn't know. He hadn't kept tabs on the strange lobbying relationship between mothers and fathers and coach Callahan. The politics between children and playing time was depressingly convoluted and dramatic. He stayed apart from it all. Apparently, Barbara had convinced Greg Callahan to put Clara in for some test runs freshman year, and due to her sheer speed, he began to use her for clears and final minute games.
Clara had the same ridiculous motor of her mother, but was even lighter more graceful when she ran. She looked like a deer on that field, and R. always imagined her on the Track, winning gold medals in the mile and the decathlon. R. didn't know whole lot about sports, but always thought the decathlon was the pinnacle of athleticism. And it of course, being the pinnacle, should accept his daughter as a ready candidate.
