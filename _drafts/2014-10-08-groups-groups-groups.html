Studying the Poincare group this week in class, and because many thoughts are bubbling up, I'd like to write down a few things.

A group is a very abstract thing that I thought was quite stupid as an undergraduate (not too long ago!). It is a set of elements, $a \epsilon G$ that are equipped with a multiplication operation -- which we call $*$ -- such that:

\begin{eqnarray}
a * b \ \epsilon \  G\ \ \ \forall a,b\  \epsilon \ G
\end{eqnarray}

Which means that a times b is an element of the group G, for all elements in G. This property means that the group is closed under multiplication. We also have that the multiplication operation is associative:

\begin{eqnarray}
a*(b*c)=(a*b)*c
\end{eqnarray}

and that there exists an identity element, such that

\begin{eqnarray}
e * a = a \forall \ a \ \epsilon G
\end{eqnarray}

We also need a proper inverse for all elements a, which send us back to the identity:

\begin{eqnarray}
a*a^{-1} = e
\end{eqnarray}

Well \textbf{that's} a lot to digest... The strange thing about these groups is that they correspond to very physical things. In particular, Lie groups are interesting, which are groups that can be ``generated'' continuously -- or connected continuously -- to the identity element.

First let's define the cyclic subgroup $\langle a \rangle$, which consists of all possible powers of the group element $a$:

\begin{eqnarray}
\langle a \rangle &=& \left[ a^{-k}, \dots, a^{-1},a^0,a^1,a^2, \dots a^n \right] \ \forall k \ \epsilon Z
\end{eqnarray}

Where that Z above, is all integers. What do I mean by subgroup? I mean a subset of G -- written $S \subset G$ -- that is itself a group. (i.e. $S$ is closed under multiplication, contains inverses of all its members and the identity, and don't forget associativity). Obviously powers of $a \in S$ map to powers of $\langle a \rangle$. This cyclic subgroup thing is very useful, because if we have a cyclic subgroup $\langle S \rangle$, created by all integer powers of some subgroup of G called S, and we can create all elements in the group $G$ using $\langle S \rangle$, then we say that the elements of $S$ are the generators of the group G. Confusing enough for you?

I guess this really means if we can build all $g \in G$ with powers of the elements of $S,e$, then those are our generators.

Now why do we care? And why did I not care as an undergraduate?
