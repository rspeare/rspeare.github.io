-----Incomplete Musings, soon to be finished...-----


             Why am I insulted by Jonathan Franzen’s commentary on the life – and death – of David Foster Wallace? Is it because I’m a hopelessly adoring reader, only capable of recognizing Wallace’s massive intellect and none of his foibles – however gracefully and endearingly he describes them – such as self-conscious solipsism, narcissism, and snooty 21rst century boredom? Is it because Franzen often writes with a self-involved, literary cant that I find repulsive? Or is it because I don’t want to hear what Franzen has to say; a man who communicates so beautifully and exposes his own foibles so recklessly, ruthlessly; just like his friend DFW. Is it because unlike Wallace, Franzen illustrates his quirks with a serious face? Is it because he lacks DFW’s care to evince readers’ self-deception and self-deprecation in a “loving”, hyperbolic manner? Is it because I suspect that Franzen is jealous; jealous of DFW’s legacy and his suicide as a calculating, unbearable career-move. I honestly don’t know.
This doesn’t mean I don’t like Franzen.
He is capable of turns of phrase and point that are entirely out of my reach. He elucidates the most delightful and inspiring conundrums, both in form and content. I envy his power. I wish I could parse life’s troubles with such a capable pen. I am in awe of the man and this piece of writing constitutes an act of presumption[1]; but as I read Franzen’s post-mortem rodomontade on “our” good friend David Foster Wallace, I am compelled to enter the fray; to cast my vote; to try to define Wallace’s life through my lens – an adoring reader.[2]
            David Foster Wallace was a godsend for me. He was the only author crazy enough, manic enough, to arrest my attention in the midst of college exams and research projects, binge-drinking parties and oncoming graduation. His intense self-contemplation, his rapidly ramifying footnotes, and auto-galvanization against every possible criticism allowed me – and my self-centered 22-year-old self – to poke fun at solipsism and its manifestation in my friends, my family, and the human race.  DFW gave me a sense of humor about the scarier things in life: a skeptic eye for the ontologically isolating, and often, embarrassing.[3]Franzen might invoke his Robinson Crusoe metaphor at this outburst of sentimentality. He might speak about radical individualism, its role in contemporary fiction and contemporary, internet-driven lives; he might say that I am grateful for the “island of David Foster Wallace”, the emissary of existential solitude. Well, I would have to agree.
Franzen describes the advent of the novel in a New Yorker article “Farther Away” and how it took Britain – and the world – by storm. In his words, a novel is “a mapping of a writer’s experience onto a waking dream”; a new piece of art that was accessible to every class, with characters drafted as “’species’ – as something more than individual, less than universal’”(Franzen on Fielding), offering a blip of emotional resonance to any reader. The novel was like crack cocaine in a country “quick to contemplate itself“ (Franzen) and chock-full of housewives who trembled in histrionics beneath domestic ennui – eager to escape their quotidian lives and enter into another [hu]man’s ‘waking dream’.[4] This yearning for transcendence applies to many British citizens of the time; and, I am no different than those housewives: I tremble beneath histrionics every week. I enjoy an unparalleled level of corporeal comfort and free time – just like the housewives and white collared workers of the industrial revolution – so great that I can be thrown into equivocating conniptions by the prospect of dinner reservations.[5]The 21rst century human is prone to maximizing, obsessively consuming, multi-tasking, and self-consciously ‘mapping’ themselves onto the Internet – props to Franzen for coining that phrase – what better author to relieve the self-conscious pressure we put upon ourselves than DFW, the master of incessant self-reflection, maximizing, recursive q.v. footnotes, the man who maps his extraordinary mental experience into a gripping, hilarious nightmare.
It was no coincidence that DFW’s books spread like wildfire after his death: the stuff was damn good and damn applicable to my generation. We all have moments of boredom in which we desire escape from cranial incarceration; David Foster Wallace was – and still is – my most effective escape. His interpretation of the novel, his shared belief with Franzen in its ability to extirpate loneliness, led to a powerful body of work that I will continually revisit in times of stress.
           
            Franzen has every right to bitterness. Wallace spurned family and friends for suicide. He had to escape, despite their best efforts, and I’m sure his choice can feel like betrayal. The problem is, some reactions should be kept private. I’m sure publishers were clamoring over Franzen for commentary on the late American-existentialist scion. I’m sure there’s a strong tug to cast your vote – as I’m doing at this moment – to define and refine the memory of a friend.[6] But, one must be careful with reactive statements. Franzen was still working through some angst and anger when he spoke at DFW’s funeral. He is still working through some professional comparison issues as he writes the 2011 article “Farther Away” for the New Yorker. Sentiment is all well and good, but keep it tempered, at least for a time.          
It is bad professional sportsmanship for Franzen to attribute DFW’s wide circulation to his suicide, or should I say, his suicide to his concomitant wide circulation: and worse, to do it publicly. We will never know if Wallace’s work would have attained its current level of fame without a horrific death.[7] People are two-faced and literary establishments – like committees, colleges, or any group that spends an inordinate amount of time running triage on its applicants – are susceptible to arbitrary choice. Let us just be thankful that we were left with DFW’s words: I certainly am. You have a wide audience Franzen, you can always rescind criticism against a friend – thanks to your literary clout and ready readers – just be careful, you might say things you’ll regret.

But back to the point: This is supposed to be encomium for David Foster Wallace – a man who taught me how to derive relief from books.

I live in a city where things are monotonous and dull. There is a miasmic, smoggy sky each day – dry and desultory – entirely without meteorological character. No wonder the inhabitants of this place imagined illusions in the desert, Jinn living behind thermal ripples of still air. Pedestrians do not walk here; they float. There is no resolute or New-York-haughty gait – except in foreigners – only the billowing amble of youth, seemingly lost in the dusty corridors of this steel city. Business moves slow here. Deals are conversational and most recreation is focused on a few empty tasks. The culture is still building – every city requires some interesting avocational germination.  I spend most my days eating, reading, writing, teaching and running. It’s incredibly dull, but wonderful, because I know how to deal with weltschmerz.
I don’t have to get drunk, or go to a play, or a movie, or watch TV, to be happy – although many of these things have a powerful place in my priveleged life – I can read a book. One of the most profound and motherly points DFW ever made was describing a good author as a person with whom you can take a walk, who can hold your hand, who can point out all his insights with a click of transitive consciousness. He, or she, is a guide, a structured and creative conversationalist, a teacher and a friend. I love books that give a visceral sense of the author, that elucidate points & feelings with accuracy and comedic grace because I love to be taught; and talked to.[8]DFW was fantastic at engendering both of those sentiments. By reading him, I have been able to find the self-effacing, human element in any piece of fiction – however abstruse (ahem, Faulkner) – because he laid out the ground rules so carefully and caringly. I am now a hopeless student of fiction because DFW gave me the eyes to see the point. Why write? To be alone without truly being alone. Why read? Ditto.
